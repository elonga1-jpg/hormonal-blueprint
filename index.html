Progesterone — the luteal hormone
🌡
Temperature: Progesterone is thermogenic — it raises core temperature by 0.3–0.6°C after ovulation. A sharp, sustained rise on your device confirms ovulation happened. A flat or noisy curve suggests it may not have.
💓
HRV: Progesterone pushes the nervous system into sympathetic mode, suppressing HRV. The WHOOP 45,000-cycle study shows HRV naturally hits its lowest around day 27. A low score pre-period is progesterone working — not a problem.
⚡
Activity: Your body redirects energy inward during the luteal phase. The same workout that felt easy two weeks ago now costs more — your steps, active minutes, or activity score reflect a body that has less to give, not one that is failing.
◎
Skin: As progesterone rises, sebum production increases. If your skin breaks out in the same week each cycle, this is the driver — especially if it's oily at the T-zone and congested around the chin.
Your actions
Workout Swap high-intensity for strength or yoga this week. Your muscles still respond well to resistance — just skip the cardio spikes.
Skin Use a salicylic acid cleanser 2–3x this week. Keep moisturiser light and non-comedogenic. Don't skip SPF — progesterone makes skin more photosensitive.
Estrogen — the follicular architect
🌡
Temperature: Estrogen keeps temperature low and stable in the first half of your cycle. A sharp dip just before ovulation is the estrogen surge triggering the LH peak. If that post-ovulatory rise is blunted, estrogen may be outpacing progesterone.
💓
HRV: Estrogen enhances parasympathetic tone — HRV is naturally highest in the follicular phase. If yours crashes hard in the luteal phase (not just dips), estrogen dominance relative to progesterone is likely the driver of that PMS pattern.
⚡
Activity: The follicular phase is your performance window. Estrogen improves glycogen use and muscle repair, so your step count, active calories, or activity scores tend to be higher here — and you recover faster between sessions.
◎
Skin: Estrogen stimulates collagen and hydration — skin peaks at ovulation. When estrogen is poorly cleared it triggers histamine and inflammation, causing cyclical puffiness, sensitivity, and breakouts in the same phase each month.
Your actions
Workout This is your window for HIIT, heavy lifts, or PBs. Push harder now — your body is primed for it and will recover well.
Skin Support clearance: eat cruciferous vegetables daily, limit alcohol, and prioritise fibre. Topically, a vitamin C serum works best now — estrogen makes your skin more receptive to brightening actives.
Androgens — the PCOS signal
🌡
Temperature: The clearest wearable sign of androgen excess is no biphasic temperature shift — the curve stays flat or noisy all month. Without ovulation there is no progesterone surge, so the expected post-ovulatory temperature rise never happens.
💓
HRV: Elevated androgens and insulin resistance increase resting sympathetic activity, chronically suppressing HRV. Unlike other hormonal patterns, this doesn't follow a cycle — it's persistently blunted regardless of phase.
⚡
Activity: Insulin resistance means your cells don't use glucose efficiently — energy crashes after meals, inconsistent output day to day, and activity that feels disproportionately hard. Your steps or active minutes may be lower than expected for the effort you're putting in.
◎
Skin & hair: Testosterone and DHT drive excess sebum, cystic jawline acne, and scalp hair thinning. These are direct readouts of androgen receptor activity — not hygiene or diet failures in isolation.
Your actions
Workout Prioritise strength training — it improves insulin sensitivity more than cardio alone. Walk after meals to blunt glucose spikes. Consistent moderate activity beats occasional intense sessions.
Skin Use a gentle salicylic or niacinamide cleanser for sebum control. Avoid heavy occlusives. Internally, spearmint tea (2 cups daily) has evidence for reducing androgens. Zinc is worth adding for DHT.
Cortisol — the cycle disruptor
🌡
Temperature: Chronic cortisol blunts overnight thermoregulation — your body can't cool down properly, disrupting deep sleep and keeping temperature elevated. On a wearable this appears as a consistently elevated deviation with no clean cyclical wave.
💓
HRV: Cortisol is the most direct HRV suppressor. Unlike progesterone's predictable mid-cycle dip, cortisol-driven suppression is persistent across all phases and doesn't improve with rest days alone — resting heart rate stays elevated too.
⚡
Activity: When cortisol is chronically high, even moderate activity days can show elevated output on your device because your baseline stress state is high. Your body is already working hard before you've moved — the activity cost compounds on top of that.
◎
Skin: Cortisol increases sebum, breaks down collagen, and triggers inflammation — oily, dull, and reactive skin that doesn't respond well to actives. It also drives central fat storage and sugar cravings, which loop back to worsen skin.
Your actions
Workout Cap intense sessions at 45 minutes and eat within 30 minutes after. Skip the fasted morning workout this week — cortisol is already high on waking. Walk instead.
Skin Focus on barrier repair: ceramide moisturiser, no harsh actives right now. Internally, prioritise sleep and eat regularly — no skipping meals. Ashwagandha (KSM-66) has strong evidence for reducing cortisol if you want a supplement.
